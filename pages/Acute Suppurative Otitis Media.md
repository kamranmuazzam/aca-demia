	- ### Causative Agents
		- *Streptococcus pyogenes* (Beta-Hemolytic streptococcus) (most common, but less common if vaccinated)
		- *S. pneumonia* (most common if vaccinated)
		- *H. influenza* (most common, especially in children)
		- *Moraxella catarrhalis* (less common)
		- *S. aureus*
	- ### Risk Factors
		- Age: Infants & Child
		- Poor Socio-economic status
		- Recurrent attacks of common cold
		- Tonsillitis/ Enlarged Adenoid (Obstructing Eustachian Tube)
		- Daycare attendance
		- Immunodeficiency
		- Smoking / Passive Smoking
		- Bottle-feeding in supine position
- ## Epidemiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Incidence
		- ~80% of children experience at least one episode by age 10
		- Peak incidence : 6-24 months
	- ### Prevalence
		- Higher in winter/spring (viral URI season)
		- ### Demographics
			- sex : More frequent in males
	- ### Geographic Distribution
		- Native American
		- Inuit Populations
		- Global, higher in low resource settings
- ## Pathophysiology #ent-prof-card #unanswered #[[Otitis Media]] #card

  id:: 782d278c-2df9-4263-8e34-97ecc224a950
  deck:: #ENT
	- ### Mechanism of Disease
		- Eustachian tube dysfunction -> negative middle ear pressure -> effusion
		- Bacterial/ viral invasion -> mucosal inflammation -> suppuration
		- TM bulging -> potential perforation -> otorrhea
	- ### Affected Systems/Organs
		- Middle ear, mastoid air cells, rarely intracranial (if complications)
	- ### Why Quite Frequent in Infants and Children #ent-prof-written #unanswered #[[Otitis Media]] #card
	  id:: 6757fa48-ff98-449d-a3ed-3ba27e8a7f71
	  deck:: #ENT
		- Because the Eustachian Tube of children is
			- Shorter
			- Narrower
			- More Horizontal
		- Immature immune system
		- Adenoid Hypertrophy (more common in children)

	- ### Clinical Presentation
		- #### Symptoms
			- Severe Otalgia (Earache, often sudden)
			- Conductive Hearing Loss (Ear Fullness)
			- Otorrhea
			- (Rhinorrhea)
			- Fever (common in children)
			- Irritability/ poor feeding (infants)
		- #### Signs
			- Erythematous/bulging TM (early)
			- Yellow/white pus behind TM (suppuration)
			- Mastoid tenderness (if mastoiditis develops)
	- ### Stages or Classification
		- Tubal occlusion : Ear fullness, retracted TM
		- Presuppuration : TM hyperemia, severe pain
		- Suppuration : TM bulging, fever
		- Resolution : Pain resolves, TM heals (or perforates)
		- Complications : Mastoiditis, meningitis, etc
- ## Diagnosis
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Diagnostic Criteria
		- Acute onset + otalgia + TM inflammation Â± effusion
	- ### Investigation
		- ### Establishing Diagnosis
			- ### Auroscopic Examination (Otoscopy)
				- [[Tympanic Membrane]] congested
				- Handle of Malleus Prominent
				  *Loss of Landmarks*
				- Pneumatic otoscopy (reduced TM mobility)
			- Tympanometry : Flat curve (effusion)
		- ### When to Image
			- CT temporal bone : if mastoiditis/intracranial spread suspected
			- MRI brain for intracranial complications (e.g., facial palsy)
		- ### Ruling Out Differential Diagnosis

			  | Referred pain (dental, TMJ) | |
			  | Malignant Otitis Externa | diabetes/immunocompromised, age, granulation tissue in EAC |

- ## Management and Treatment #ent-prof-card #ent-prof-written #unanswered #[[Otitis Media]] #card
  background-color:: blue
  id:: b1c1548a-73e5-4d11-9249-1f0f1b2b101e
  deck:: #ENT
	- ### Medical Treatments
		- Antibacterial Therapy
			- First line : Amoxicillin (or Amoxiclav for resistance)
			- Alternatives : Azithromycin, cefdinir (if penicillin allergy)
		- Nasal Decongentants
			- Decongestant Nasal Drops
			- Oral Nasal Decongestants
				- Pseudoephedrine + Antihistamine (if combined, then nasal drops not required)
		- Analgesics and Antipyretics
		- Dry Local Heat
	- ### Surgical Options
		- Ear Toilet (suctioning discharge)
		- [[Myringotomy]]
	- ### Lifestyle and Dietary Recommendations
		- Keep Ear Dry
	- ### Follow-up
		- Recheck at 10 days (ensure resolution)
			- Change Antibiotics
		- Persistent Effusion > 12 weeks -> [[Glue Ear]] Management 
		  *Periodic check for 12 weeks*
		- carefully followed till tympanic membrane returns to its normal appearance and conductive hearing loss disappears.
	- ### Treatment of ASOM In Post Perforative Stage #ent-prof-written #unanswered #[[Otitis Media]] #card
	  id:: 6757faf1-6140-42af-a322-da47ed1398b6
	  deck:: #ENT
		- Aural Toilet
			- Gentle suctioning or mopping of discharge under microscopy
			- Avoid aggressive cleaning (risk of further trauma)
		- Topical Antibiotics
			- First line : Quinoline ear drops (e.g., Ofloxacin 0.3% or Ciprofloxacin 0.2%)
				- Rationale : Effective against Pseudomonas and other ASOM pathogens; safe for middle ear
				- Avoid ototoxic drops
				  *Aminoglycosides like Gentamicin*
		- Systemic Antibiotics
			- Systemic symptoms(fever malaise)
			- No imporovement with topical therapy
			- Options
				- Amoxiclav
				- Macrolides (Azithromycin)
		- Dry Ear Precautions
		- Analgesia/Antipyretics : NSAIDs
		- Follow up
		  *1-2 weeks*
			- COnfirm perforation closure (most heal spontaneously)
			- If persistent discharge -> reassess for CSOM or cholestatoma
		- *Most case heal spontaneously with proper care, Chronic/recurrent cases may require tympanoplasty*
- ## Prognosis
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Expected Course
		- #### With Treatment
			- 90% resolve within 7-10 days
		- #### Without Treatment
			- Spontaneous Resolution : 70% within 7-14 days
			  *watchful waiting is often advised for 48-72 hours and analgesics/antipyretics*
