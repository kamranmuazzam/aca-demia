- #definition Congenital digit duplication (extra fingers/toes)
- One of the most common congenital limb anomalies
- ## Etiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Causative Agents
		- Sporadiac (most common) or familial (autosomal dominant with variable penetrance)
	- ### Risk Factors
		- Associated with syndromes (e.g., Ellis-van Creveld, McKusick-Kaufman, Bardet Biedl)

- ## Clinical Features
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Clinical Presentation
		- #### Symptoms
			- Visible extra digit at birth (may be rudimentary or fully formed)
			- May affect hand function (if unstable or malaligned)
		- #### Signs
	- ### Stages or Classification
		- Based on Location
			- Preaxial (Radial/ Tibila side)
				- Thumb/ big toe duplication
			- Postaxial (Ulnar? Fibular side)
				- Little finger/ toe duplication
			- Central
				- Rare, involes 2nd, 3rd or 4th digits
			- Based on Complexity
				- Simple : Soft tissue only
				- Complex : Involves bone, joint or nail
- ## Diagnosis
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Diagnostic Criteria
		- Clinical Examination
		- X-ray (assess bone involvement & articulation)
		- Genetic testing if syndromic suspicion*
		  Always evaluate for associated syndromes, especially in bilateral or complex cases.*

- ## Management and Treatment
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Surgical Options
	  *if functionally/aesthetically concerning*
		- Surgical excision
			- if functionally/aesthetically concerning
				- Simple : Suture ligation in neonates (for pedunculated digits)
				- Complex : Reconstruction of ligaments, tendons, and bones
			- Timing : Usually done at 6-12 months (earlier if pedunculated)
