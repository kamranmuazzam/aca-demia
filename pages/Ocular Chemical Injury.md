- ## Etiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Causative Agents
		- Alkalis
		  *liquifactive necrosis, penetrate deeper, more severe*
			- Ammonia
			- Caustic Potash
			- Caustic Soda
			- NaOH
			- Lime
		- Acids
		  *coagulative necrosis, may limit penetration*
			- Sulfuric Acid
			- Hydrochloric acid
		- Irritants & Dye
		  *milder injury*
			- Solvents
			- Detergents
			- Tear gas
			- Dye
	- ### Risk Factors
		- Occupational exposure (industrial, laboratory, cleaning agents)
		- Accidental splashes (household chemicals, constructions materials)
		- Assault (intentional chemical exposure)
		- lack of eye protection
- ## Epidemiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Incidence
		- 7-18% of ocular traumas worldwide
		- More common in industrial/agricultural settings
	- ### Prevalence
		- ### Demographics
			- More common in males (work-related exposure)
			- Bimodal distribution: young adults (occupational) & elderly (household accidents)
	- ### Geographic Distribution
		- Higher in areas with poor workplace safety regulations
- ## Pathophysiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Mechanism of Disease
		- Alkalis: Saponification of cell membranes -> liquifactive necrosis -> deeper penetration (anterior chamber damage)
		- Acids : Protein denaturation -> Coagulation necrosis (barrier limits penetration)
		- Secondary Inflammation -> corneal opacification, limbal stem cell deficiency
	- ### Affected Systems/Organs
		- Cornea (epithelial loss, stromal melting)
		- Conjunctiva (Ischemia, necrosis)
		- Limbus (stem cell damage -> Conjunctivalization)
		- Anterior chamber (alkali-induced trabecular meshwork damage -> glaucoma)
- ## Clinical Features
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Clinical Presentation
		- #### Symptoms
			- Severe pain, photophobia
			- Blurred vision
			- Foreign body sensation
			- Eyelid swelling
		- #### Signs
			- Conjunctival injection/chemosis
			- Corneal epithelial defects (fluorescein staining)
			- Limbal ischemia (whitening, vessel occlusion)
			- Anterior chamber reaction (cells/flare)
			- Associated Findings per grade
				- Grade I/II : Mild stromal edema, no anterior chamber reaction
				- Grade III/IV
					- Corneal melting
					- Secondary glaucoma (trabecular damage)
					- Sympblepharon (conjunctival scarring)
	- ### Stages or Classification
		- Roper-Hall(1965) Classification (based on cornea clarity & limbal ischemia)
			- Grade I (good prognosis) -> Grade IV (bad prognosis)
				- | **Grade** | **Corneal Signs**            | **Limbal Ischemia** | **Prognosis** |  
				    |-----------|-------------------------------|---------------------|---------------|  
				    | I         | Clear                         |(Mild hyperemia, no blanching.) None                | Excellent     |  
				    | II        | Hazy (**iris visible**)       | (Patchy blanching)<1/3                | Good          |  
				    | III       | **Iris obscured** (ground glass) | (Confluent Blanching)1/3–1/2          | Guarded       |  
				    | IV        | Opaque (**no iris/pupil view**) | (Total Limbal Ischemia)>1/2   + Scleral involvement          | Poor          |
		- Dua(2001) Classification (based on conjunctival involvement)
			- | **Grade** | **Limbal Ischemia** | **Conjunctival Loss** |  
			    |-----------|---------------------|-----------------------|  
			    | V         | >6 clock-hours      | >50%                 |  
			    | VI        | Total               | Total                 |
- ## Diagnosis
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Diagnostic Criteria
		- History of chemical exposure + clinical signs
	- ### Investigation
		- ### Establishing Diagnosis
			- Slit-lamp exam (corneal integrity, anterior chamber)
			- pH testing (irrigate first if needed)
			- Fluorescein staining (epithelial defects)
		- ### Ruling Out Differential Diagnosis
			- |DD|Ruling Out|
			  |--|--|
			  |Thermal burn|History of heat/fire exposure|
			  |Infectious keratitis| Gradual onset, microbial stains|
			  |UV keratitis| History of welding/UV exposure|
		- ### General Assessment
			- Visual acuity
			- Intraocular pressure (secondary glaucoma risk)
- ## Management and Treatment
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Medical Treatment
		- Immediate irrigation (30+ minutes with saline/water)
		- Topical antibiotics (fluoroquinolones)
		- Cycloplegics (atropine) for pain
		- Ascorbic acid/citrate (for alkali burns)
			- Sodium Citrate 10%
		- Artificial Tear Drops
		- Steroids (controversial; avoid in acute necrosis)
	- ### Surgical Options
	  Grade III/IV
		- Amniotic membrane transplant
		- Tenoplasty (for severe limbal ischemia)
		- Keratoplasty (late-stage)
	- ### Lifestyle and Dietary Recommendations
		- Vitamin C supplementation (collagen synthesis)
	- ### Follow-up
		- Frequent monitoring for corneal melting/glaucoma

- ## Key Literature
  background-color:: blue
	- *Roper-Hall MJ (1965)*: Emphasized **volume > pH adjustment** for initial treatment. (to use Bicarbonate)
	- *AAO Guidelines (2023)*: No strong recommendation for bicarbonate; saline remains standard.
	  Bicarbonate risks corneal edema if used excessively (hypertonic)
	- *Wagoner et al., Surv Ophthalmol (1997) – Alkali injury mechanisms*
