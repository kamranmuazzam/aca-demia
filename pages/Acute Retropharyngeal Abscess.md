- ## Etiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Causative Agents #ent-prof-written #unanswered #card
	  id:: 25b9a42f-a66c-4373-9430-b840179b9c7e
	  deck:: #ENT
		- **Bacterial:**
			- *Streptococcus pyogenes* (Group A Streptococcus)
			- *Staphylococcus aureus* (including MRSA)
			- Anaerobes (*Fusobacterium*, *Bacteroides*, *Peptostreptococcus*)
			- *Klebsiella pneumoniae* (in immunocompromised/diabetics)
			- *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (in endemic regions)
			- *Haemophilus influenzae* (rare post-vaccination)
		- **Viral:** Rare as primary cause, but EBV/adenovirus may precede bacterial infection.
	- ### Risk Factors
		- **Pediatric age** (<5–6 years, due to retropharyngeal lymph nodes)
		- **Recent URI/pharyngitis**
		- **Trauma** (foreign body, intubation, endoscopic procedures)
		- **Immunocompromised states** (HIV, diabetes, chemotherapy)
		- **Dental infections/procedures**
		- **GERD/chronic irritation**
- ## Epidemiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Incidence
		- Rare in adults; more common in children (peak: 2-4 years)
		- Estimated 1-3 cases per 100,000 in pediatric populations
	- ### Prevalence
		- Higher in unvaccinated populations (e.g., H. influenzae)
		- ### Demographics
			- Children(lymph node-realted)
			- Adult(trauma/immunocompromise)
	- ### Geographic Distribution
		- No specific regional predominance
- ## Pathophysiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Mechanism of Disease
		- Infection spreads to retropharyngeal lymph nodes -> suppuration -> abscess formation
		- In adults, often due to direct trauma or extension from adjacent infections (e.g., odontogenic)
	- ### Affected Systems/Organs
		- Retropharyngeal space (between buccopharyngeal fascia and alar fascia)
		- Potential mediastinal extension (via danger space)
		- Airway compromise due to mass defect
- ## Clinical Features #ent-prof-card #ent-prof-written #unanswered #card
  background-color:: blue
  id:: 27392bbe-50d1-4650-837d-dccf4f455d4f
  deck:: #ENT
	- ### Clinical Presentation
		- #### Symptoms
			- Severe sore throat (odynophagia)
			- Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing)
			- Fever, malaise
			- Stridor or respiratory distress (if airway compromised)
			- Neck stiffness (mimicking meningitis)
		- #### Signs
			- Fluctuant Bulging posterior pharyngeal wall (may not be visible in early stages)
			- Torticollis ("cock-robin" posture)
			- Cervical lymphadenopathy
			- Drooling(due to dysphagia)
	- ### Stages or Classification
		- Cellulitis(early) -> Abscess(late)
- ## Diagnosis
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Diagnostic Criteria
		- Clinical suspicion + Imaging confirmation (XRay/CT/MRI)
	- ### Investigation
	   #ent-prof-card #unanswered #card
	  deck:: #ENT
		- ### Establishing Diagnosis
			- CT neck with contrast (gold standard)
			- Lateral neck X-ray
				- ### Typical Radiological Features #ent-prof-written #unanswered #card
				  id:: 67581125-937a-4643-916e-2a9d4f1d223a
				  deck:: #ENT
					- widened retropharyngeal space >7mm at C2, or >14mm at C6
					- Widening of prevertebral soft tissue shadow due to presence of abscess
					- Soft tissue shadow encroaching the air column
			- Ultrasound (for pediatric bedside assessment)
		- ### Ruling Out Differential Diagnosis
			- [[Chronic Retropharyngeal Abscess]]
				- Differences #ent-prof-card #unanswered #card
				  id:: 6756ae63-85c1-4975-92c6-81bccebb2bfb
				  deck:: #ENT
					- | **Feature** | **Acute Retropharyngeal Abscess** | **Chronic Retropharyngeal Abscess** |
					  | ---- | ---- | ---- |
					  | **Onset** | Rapid (hours to days) | Gradual (weeks to months) |
					  | **Cause** | Bacterial infection (e.g., *Staphylococcus*, *Streptococcus*) | Often tuberculosis (TB), fungal infections, or atypical mycobacteria |
					  | **Symptoms** | Severe throat pain, fever, dysphagia, stridor, neck stiffness | Mild pain, low-grade fever, weight loss, chronic cough |
					  | **Progression** | Life-threatening airway obstruction if untreated | Slow progression, less acute danger |
					  | **Imaging (CT/MRI)** | Well-defined fluid collection with rim enhancement | May show necrotic nodes, calcifications (in TB) |
					  | **Treatment** | Emergency drainage, IV antibiotics, airway management | Long-term antibiotics (e.g., anti-TB drugs), drainage if needed |
					  | **Complications** | Mediastinitis, sepsis, airway compromise | Fistula formation, vertebral involvement (Pott’s disease) |
			- |DD|Ruling Out|
			  |--|--|
			  |Epiglottitis|No drooling, abrupt onset (X-ray helps)|
			  |Peritonsillar abscess| Unilateral tonsillar bulge, trismus|
			  |Meningitis| No Kernig/Brudzinski signs, LP normal|
		- ### General Assessment
			- CBC(leukocytosis)
			- Blood culture (if sepsis suspected)
- ## Management and Treatment #ent-prof-card #ent-prof-written #unanswered #card
  background-color:: blue
  id:: 32ecf5df-62d9-4669-bf18-94d0b5189c9d
  deck:: #ENT
	- ### Medical Treatment
		- IV antibiotics(empiric)
			- Ampicillin-sulbactam or Clindamycin+Vancomycin (if MRSA suspected)
		- Steroids(controversial, may reduce edema)
	- ### Surgical Options
		- Incision & drainage (transoral/transcervical) for large abscesses
			- Abscess size ≥ 2.5-3 cm
			- Ring-enhancing fluid collection
			- Gas within the abscess
			- Extension into danger/mediastinal space
		- Airway management (intubation/tracheostomy if severe obstruction)
	- ### Lifestyle and Dietary Recommendations
		- Soft diet until swallowing improves
	- ### Follow-up
		- Repeat imaging if no improvement in 48h
- ## Prognosis
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Expected Course
		- #### With Treatment
			- Resolution in 7-10 days with antibiotics ± drainage
		- #### Without Treatment
			- ##### Possible Complications
				- Airway obstruction
				- mediastinitis
				- sepsis
				- death
	- ### Survival Rates
		- #### With Treatment
			- ≥95% with timely treatment
		- #### Without Treatment
			- High mortality if untreated(due to mediastinitis)
