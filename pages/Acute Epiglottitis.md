- ## Etiology #ent-prof-card #unanswered #card
  background-color:: blue
  deck:: #ENT

	- ### Causative Agents
		- *Haemophilus influenzae* type b (Hib) (most common in unvaccinated individuals)
		- *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Sterptococcus pyogenes*
		- Viral pathogens (e.g., HSV, EBV) in immunocompromised patients
	- ### Risk Factors
		- Unvaccinated status (Hib vaccine)
		- Immunosuppression
		- Smoking or inhaled injury
		- Trauma to throat
- ## Epidemiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Incidence
		- Rare in countries with routine Hib vaccination (0.6-1 cases per 100,000 adults/year)
		- Higher in unvaccinated populations
	- ### Prevalence
		- ### Demographics
			- More common in adults(now) due to pediatric vaccination programs
				- *previously children age group 2-7 years*
	- ### Geographic Distribution
		- Lower incidence in high-income countries with vaccination programs
- ## Pathophysiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Mechanism of Disease
		- Bacterial invasion -> rapid inflammation of epiglottis, aryepiglottic folds, and supraglottic structures
		- Swelling can lead to airway obstruction
	- ### Affected Systems/Organs
		- Upper respiratory tract (epiglottis, larynx)
- ## Clinical Features #ent-prof-card #unanswered #card

  deck:: #ENT

	- ### Clinical Presentation
		- #### Symptoms
			- Sudden-onset, rapid progression-
			- Adults
				- Sore Throat
				- Dysphagia
			- Children
				- Dyspnoea
				- Stridor
				- *Rapidly progressive and may prove fatal unless relieved*
			- Fever may go upto 40ÂºC. It is due to septicaemia. Patient's condition may rapidly deteriorate.
		- #### Signs
			- red swollen epiglottis
			- Instrumentation of the oropharynx/larynx is avoided
			  *Indirect laryngoscopy may show oedema and congestion of supraglottic structure*
			- Tripod positioning (sitting forward, neck extended)
			- "Thumb sign" on lateral neck X-ray (swollen epiglottits)
	- ### Stages or Classification
		- Early (mild swelling) vs. Late (severe airway obstruction)
- ## Diagnosis
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Diagnostic Criteria
		- Clinical suspicion + imaging/endoscopy confirmation
	- ### Investigation
		- ### Establishing Diagnosis
			- Lateral neck X-ray ("thumb sign)
			- Fiberoptic laryngoscopy (gold standard, but risky in unstable patients)
			- CT neck (if diagnosis uncertain)
		- ### Ruling Out Differential Diagnosis
			- |Trait|Acute Epiglottitis|Acute Laryngo-tracheo-bronchitis|
			  |--|--|--|
			  |Causative Organism|Haemophilus influenzae|Parainfluenza virus|
			  |Age|2-7 years|3 months to 3 years|
			  |Pathology|Supralottic larynx|Subglottic area|
			  |Prodromal symptoms|Absent|Present|
			  |Onset|Sudden|Slow|
			  |Fever|High|Low grade or no fever|
			  |Patient's look|Toxic|Nontoxic|
			  |Cough|usually absent|present|
			  |Stridor|Present and may be marked|Present|
			  |Odynophagia|Present, with drooling of secretions|Usually absent|
			  |Radiology|"Thumb Sign"|Steeple sign on anteroposterior view of neck|
			  |Treatment|Humidified oxygen, third generation cephalosporin(ceftriaxone) or amoxicillin|Humidified oxygen tent, steroids|
			- |DD|Ruling Out|
			  |--|--|
			  |Croup|No drooling, barking cough|
			  |Peritonsillar abscess| Assymetric tonsillar swelling|
			  |Foreign body | History of choking |
		- ### General Assessment
			- Monitor for respiratory obstruction

	- ### Medical Treatment
	  *Hospitalisation*
		- Antibiotics
			- Third generation cephalosporin, IV/IM (empiric)
		- Steroids
			- Hydrocortisone/Dexamethason IM/TV
		- Parenteral fluids for hydration
		- Oxygen
	- ### Surgical Options
	  *if respiratory obstruction*
		- Intubation or tracheostomy
	- ### Lifestyle and Dietary Recommendations
		- Humidification
