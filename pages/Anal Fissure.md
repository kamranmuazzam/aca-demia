- #definition an anal fissure is a longitudinal ulcer in the endoderm of the distal anal canal which extends from the anal verge proximally towards but not beyond the dentate line
- ## Etiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Causative Agents
		- Trauma from hard stools
		- Chronic diarrhoea
		- Anal stretching (childbirth, anal intercourse)
	- ### Risk Factors
		- Constipation
		- Low-fibre diet
		- Dehydration
		- Infalmmatory bowel disease (Chron's disease)
		- Hypertonic anal sphincter
- ## Epidemiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Incidence
	- ### Prevalence
		- Affect ~10% of the general population
		- ### Demographics
			- Common in young-middle aged adults
			- Equal gender distribution
	- ### Geographic Distribution
		- Worldwide, higher prevalence in regions with low-fiber diets
- ## Pathophysiology
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Mechanism of Disease
		- Tear in the anoderm (sensitive squamous epithelium)
		- Ishchemia due to increased anal sphincter pressure
		- Poor healing due to repeated trauma
	- ### Affected Systems/Organs
		- Gastrointestinal (nal canal)
		- Nervous (pain due to exposed nerve endings)
- ## Clinical Features
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Clinical Presentation
		- #### Symptoms
			- Pain during defecation
			  *acute*
			- Bright red Per rectal bleeding (on toilet paper or stool surface)
			- Anal Itching or spasm
			- Mucous Discharge
			- Constipation
		- #### Signs
			- Visible tear on inspection (usually posterior midline)
			- Sentinel skin tag (chronic fissures)
	- ### Stages or Classification
		- Acute Anal Fissure (<6 weeks, superficial)
		- Chronic Anal Fissure (>6 weeks, indurated edges, exposed sphincter fibers)
- ## Diagnosis
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Diagnostic Criteria
		- Clinical History + Visual Inspection (avoid digital exam if severe pain)
	- ### Investigation
		- ### Establishing Diagnosis
			- Anoscopy (f tolerated)
			  *under anesthesia*
			- Flexible sigmoidoscopy (if IBD suspected)
		- ### Ruling Out Differential Diagnosis
			- |DD|Ruling Out|
			  |--|--|
			  |[[Hemorrhoids]]|No prolapse, bleeding less severe|
			  |Anal fistual| No discharge or recurrent abscess|
			  |Crohn's disease| Look for perianal tags, granulomas|
		- ### General Assessment
			- Rule out STIs (e.g., syphillis, herpes) if atypical presentation
- ## Management and Treatment
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Medical Treatment
		- Topical nitroglycerin (0.2-0.4%) (reduces sphincter spasm)
		- Calcium channel blockers (diltiazem gel)
		- Stool softeners (e.g., PEG, docusate)
		- High-fiber diet + hydration
	- ### Surgical Options
		- Laterral internal Spincterotomy (LIS) (gold standard for chronic fissures)
		- Botulinum toxin injection (temporary relief)
	- ### Lifestyle and Dietary Recommendations
		- Fiber supplementation (25-30 g/day)
		- Sitz baths (warm water, 10-15 mins, 2-3x/day)
	- ### Follow-up
		- Reassess in 4-6 weeks for healing
- ## Prognosis
  background-color:: blue
	- ### Expected Course
		- #### With Treatment
			- Acute fissures : Heals in 2-4 weeks
			- Chronic fissures : 90% heals with sphincterotomy
		- #### Without Treatment
			- ##### Possible Complications
				- Chronic pain
				- Anal stenosis
				- Fistula formation (rare)
			- ##### Long-Term Effects
	- ### Quality of Life Considerations
		- Impact quality of life
